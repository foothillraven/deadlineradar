The Most Common CPA License-Renewal Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

Most CPA license lapses aren't dramatic — they're small, avoidable mix-ups. Here are the ones that come up again and again.

1. Assuming every state renews on the same cycle. Some states renew annually, some every two years, some every three. Missouri's individual license renews every two years; Missouri's firm permits renew every single year — a different cycle length for the same board, in the same state (Mo. Code Regs. Ann. tit. 20 §§ 2010-2.070(1), 2010-2.072(2)). If you assume your firm follows the same clock as your individual license, you can be wrong by a full year.

2. Confusing a fixed calendar date with a birth-month or cohort system. Some states — Texas is a well-known example — renew each individual CPA annually, by the last day of their own birth month, not a single date that applies to everyone (Texas State Board of Public Accountancy, tsbpa.texas.gov/cpas/renewal/). If you're used to a fixed-date state and move to or get licensed in a birth-month state, assuming a single statewide date is a fast way to miss your actual deadline.

3. Missing the CPE deadline while celebrating the license renewal. Covered in more depth in our CPE vs. license renewal piece — the short version is: renewing your license does not automatically mean your CPE hours are current, or that CPE is even due at the same time. Check both, separately, every cycle.

4. Trusting a stale reminder from a state board, an employer, or a CPE vendor. Some state boards do send their own renewal reminders — but not all of them do, and the ones that do vary in reliability and lead time. Relying solely on someone else to remember for you is a single point of failure. A reminder tied to the actual published deadline, not a third party's internal process, is safer.

5. Waiting until the late-renewal grace window and assuming there's no real consequence. Several states publish a formal late-renewal period with an added fee (Missouri's late window ran through December 31 for its most recent cycle, per the Board's own 2026 Winter newsletter) — but a grace period is not a second deadline. It's a penalty window, and after it closes, reinstatement is usually harder and more expensive than a normal renewal.

The fix for all five: know your specific state's exact rule (cycle length, fixed date vs. birth-month vs. cohort, and whether CPE has its own separate deadline), and track it with a real reminder tied to the actual date — not a guess, a memory, or someone else's process. Look up your state here.
